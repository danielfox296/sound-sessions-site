I'm not a skeptic, and I'm not a believer

A music producer's answer to the question the category can't answer honestly: which claims are false, what's actually true, and why the hour is still worth having.

Firstwater Denver · 2026-07-22 · 6 min read

Does sound healing work? It depends entirely on what someone told you it does. If a facilitator promised that a bowl would repair your cells or dissolve a disease, no. That is not happening in a room with a bowl in it. If you came for an uninterrupted hour to sit with your own mind, near other people doing the same thing, then probably, and that has almost nothing to do with the chemistry of the sound.

I make records for a living, and I have wanted to walk out of more than one sound bath. I also run my own. Both of those are true, and they sit together more easily than either the people selling this or the people mocking it tend to admit.

Is sound healing real, or is it pseudoscience? #

A few years ago I was at a sound bath in Kelowna, British Columbia, on a Sunday morning. The practitioner told the room that the sound targeted diseased cells and either exploded them or rendered them inert. I got suspicious before she finished the sentence.

That specific claim, that tones played across a room kill disease, has nothing under it. Tones are everywhere. You are soaking in them right now. A lot of evidence would be needed to show that ordinary sound does something medical to the body, and that evidence is not on the table. Extraordinary claims still require extraordinary evidence, and this corner of the wellness world hands out extraordinary claims for the price of a ticket.

Can sound actually kill cancer cells? #

Here is the strange part. There is a real thing underneath the Kelowna claim, and it is more interesting than the claim.

Researchers at Caltech published work on something they named oncotripsy: you can tune ultrasound to a frequency where cancer cells and healthy cells respond differently, and at that frequency the cancer cells come apart while the healthy ones are left intact. It is real, it is careful, and it is nowhere near a yoga studio. The experiments run in a dish, on cells in suspension, using ultrasound around 500,000 hertz.

A singing bowl rings somewhere near 250 to 500 hertz. The ultrasound in those experiments sits near 500,000. That is roughly eleven octaves up. Eleven octaves is the whole distance between an experimental oncology technique and a person waving a mallet on a Sunday. When a facilitator borrows the headline and drops the eleven octaves, the room gets a costume instead of the science.

This is the part that actually bothers me, and it is not the belief. It is the accuracy. People can get something real out of this hour, and the whole thing becomes a laughing stock the moment someone claims a bowl cured cancer. That claim alienates every reasonable person in earshot, and it takes the people who came in good faith down with it.

Cancer recovery credited to breath, meditation, lifestyle, and a strong held belief over months? I can entertain that. Cancer recovery from tones? No. The difference matters, and blurring it is how the category earns its worst reputation.

So does a sound bath do anything? #

I stayed on the mat in Kelowna. I wasn’t there for apoptosis. I was there to relax, to work with my own attention, to spend an hour I would have otherwise wasted around good people, and to hear whatever the facilitator’s instinct decided I should hear.

That is the honest offer, and I think of it as an asymmetrical bet. You stake forty-five dollars and an hour. Compare that hour to the others in your week. Therapy is an hour. A pointless work call is an hour. Being on hold with the DMV is an hour. Every one of them is a chance to observe yourself and work with what you find, and every one of them is shredded by distraction, aggravation, and other people pushing their demands into your attention.

A sound bath is an increasingly rare thing: a room curated on purpose, in sound and light and intention, that hands you uninterrupted time to settle your breath, put down some fear or judgment or old story, and practice being the person you would rather be, next to other people doing the same work. The alternative for that hour is usually scrolling a feed built to spike your cortisol as efficiently as possible. Set against that, the bet is not close.

What if I go and nothing happens? #

Sometimes people leave underwhelmed and turn it on themselves. There’s a real post out there from a first-timer: “I left the experience feeling something is wrong with me or my energy is stale.” Nothing moved for her, and she assumed the fault was hers.

It wasn’t. If a friend tells you a drive in the country will clear your head, and you spend the whole drive stuck in traffic, that is not a character flaw. Sound, space, community, and rest are the elements here. We are responsible for working with them, and we hold them loosely, because they don’t perform on command. Someone arriving with a racing, unpracticed mind may feel nothing at the end of an hour. That same person can come back on a different day, in a different season, and finally let go of tension their body has held for years.

Each session is an opportunity, not a guarantee. To borrow a phrase from the Christian tradition, it works something like a means of grace. You set yourself up, through your own choices and attention, to grow. Church and prayer work the same way. The remarkable thing doesn’t always arrive. And sometimes it does.

How do I tell honest facilitation from snake oil? #

A facilitator’s ideas and the truth are two separate things, and you are allowed to hold them separately. Somebody hitting a gong intuitively is not thereby handed the secrets of the universe. Some of them might have real wisdom. That is for you to weigh.

My rule is sovereignty. Bring your own inner knowing and your own calibrated judgment, especially to any claim that sounds fantastical, and let the rest wash past. Not everything said in a sound bath needs to be put on trial. It isn’t a court of law, and if you feel it has to be, there may be more freedom available in letting other people hold their own ideas beside your skepticism without it costing you the room. You can dismiss the claim and keep the hour. I do it all the time.

So: does sound healing work? The cellular claims don’t, and I’ll say that as plainly as anyone with a lab coat. The hour does, for a lot of people, most of the time, for reasons that need no pseudoscience to explain. Come in expecting the second thing and you will rarely leave empty-handed.

I stayed on the mat in Kelowna. I wasn’t there for the cure. The hour was good.
